Wainwright EQUITY RESEARCH 02 — MY STORY From the lab bench to ecosystem building. "I did not come into biotech through finance. I came into it through the lab." My path started early. Long before I understood venture capital, I understood what disease could take from a family. That experience made biology personal for me. It pushed me into laboratories as a teenager and later into stem cell science, where I became obsessed with one question: can we move medicine from treatment toward repair? I started in stem cell labs in New York — at Mount Sinai, Weill Cornell, Vanderbilt, and then the New York Stem Cell Foundation — because I believed biology could be engineered toward regeneration and ultimately cures. The work was foundational: developing iPSC protocols that generated a patent and a publication, building disease models that led to clinical trials for Giant Axonal Neuropathy. Qatar came next. As the youngest scientist appointed to establish Qatar's first national stem cell core facility — under Qatar Foundation and Sidra Medicine — I learned what it means to build scientific infrastructure in a place that is determined to build from scratch. That experience shaped my understanding of sovereign ambition in medicine. Oxford sharpened everything. As Sir Magdi Yacoub Fellow — funded through a special award from H.H. Sheikha Moza bint Nasr and Qatar Foundation — I completed a DPhil in cardiovascular pharmacology and stem cell biology, working under Sir Magdi Yacoub, Derek Terrar, and in collaboration with Lorenz Studer at Sloan Kettering. The thesis was a new human stem cell platform for atrial cardiomyocytes. The deeper lesson was that great science, alone, is never enough. Over time that question became larger. I realised the bottleneck was not only scientific discovery. It was translation — who funds the science, who structures the company, who protects the IP, who guides the regulatory path, and who builds the ecosystem around it. They will build the infrastructure to discover, finance, manufacture, test, and deploy them. National biomedical advantage is not an accident. It is built — through deliberate investment in talent, infrastructure, capital, governance, and the global partnerships that allow local science to reach global markets. What sovereign partners need to build enduring biomedical advantage. I have spent time working inside Qatar's early stem cell infrastructure, advising GCC sovereign investors and family offices, and building investment platforms that operate across the U.S., Europe, and the Gulf. This is what I have learned. 01 A credible scientific thesis — rooted in the biology that is most tractable and most likely to produce durable advantage. 02 A fund or investment platform — to deploy capital systematically with the governance structures institutional LPs trust. 03 Clinical and translational infrastructure — hospitals, patient registries, and clinical trial capabilities that attract global science to local ground. 04 Academic and research partnerships — with Oxford, Harvard, MIT, Karolinska, and others that create the intellectual engine for home-grown discovery. 05 Company creation capability — the ability to build new ventures around local science, not only invest into foreign ones. 06 Talent attraction and retention — building the ecosystem attractive enough that the world's best scientists and entrepreneurs choose to build here. 07 Global venture and pharma connectivity — so that local companies can access the syndicates, partnerships, and markets that determine clinical and commercial success. 08 Governance that protects capital and reputation — because institutional credibility, once lost, is almost impossible to rebuild.
